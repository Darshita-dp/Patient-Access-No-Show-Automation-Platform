# Fairness Audit — No-Show Risk Model

> **Scope.** This is a **portfolio-level fairness audit on synthetic data**. It
> is intended to **identify potential disparities**, not to certify the model
> as fair or production-ready. Subgroup performance should be reviewed on real
> target-population data before any production use. The model is trained on
> the synthetic dataset generated by `etl/`; results here reflect that
> synthetic population's structure, not any real health system.

## What was audited

The shipped model (`models/no_show_model.pkl`) was evaluated against the exact
temporal test split used in training — the last 20% of appointments by date
(n = 6,400). Performance was then sliced by:

- **Gender** (F / M)
- **Age group** (0-12, 13-17, 18-30, 31-50, 51-64, 65+)
- **Clinic** (all 6 clinics)
- **Scholarship / social-program status** (proxy for socioeconomic vulnerability)
- **Risk category** (Low / Medium / High)

For every subgroup we report group size, actual no-show rate, average predicted
risk, high-risk assignment rate, precision, recall, false-positive rate, and
false-negative rate. Subgroups whose recall differs from the overall recall by
≥ 5 percentage points, or whose average predicted risk differs from their true
no-show rate by ≥ 5 percentage points, are flagged for human review. These
flags are **not certification thresholds** — they exist so a reviewer's eye
lands on the rows that matter.

## Why these metrics, in this order

Because the intended intervention is **assistive** — an extra reminder, a
short confirmation call, or a transportation check — the operational harm of a
false positive is small (a wasted 30-second call), while a false negative is a
missed appointment and a lost slot. That framing changes which fairness
metrics matter most:

1. **Equal opportunity (recall parity)** — is the model catching genuinely
   at-risk patients in every subgroup, or is one group being systematically
   under-served by outreach?
2. **Calibration within subgroups** — does an average predicted risk of 20%
   for a subgroup match that subgroup's actual 20% no-show rate, or is the
   score biased for some groups?
3. **Selection-rate parity** — is monitored, but *unequal selection that
   tracks unequal true no-show rates is not itself unfair* in an assistive
   context. What matters is whether unequal selection tracks unequal *truth*.
4. **Precision parity** — matters most as a check on staff-time equity, since
   lower precision in one group means that group absorbs more unnecessary calls.

## Findings by subgroup

### Gender — no material disparity
Recall is 63% for female patients vs. 58% for male, well inside the flag
threshold. Calibration gaps are ≤ 1.2 pp for both. **No action needed** on
this axis on the synthetic data.

### Age group — the largest disparity
This is the audit's most important finding. Recall drops sharply with age:

| Age group | n | Actual no-show | Recall | Notes |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| 0-12 (child) | 173 | 38.2% | 0.80 | Slightly over-predicts risk (-4.9 pp calibration gap) |
| 13-17 (adolescent) | 317 | 26.8% | 0.84 | Highest recall; over-predicts risk (+4.2 pp) |
| 18-30 (young adult) | 1,463 | 25.6% | 0.78 | Model works well |
| 31-50 (adult) | 2,385 | 19.8% | 0.57 | Baseline group |
| 51-64 (older adult) | 1,034 | 12.5% | 0.38 | Recall well below overall |
| 65+ (senior) | 1,028 | 11.1% | 0.22 | **Recall of 22% vs. 61% overall** |

**Interpretation.** The model is much better at catching no-shows in younger
patients. For seniors, roughly **78% of actual no-shows are missed** — the
model rarely puts them in the High-risk band, so they rarely receive proactive
outreach. On synthetic data this is partly a base-rate effect (seniors miss
less often), but it is still a concrete gap in *equal opportunity of
intervention* and would need explicit action before production use (e.g. a
group-aware threshold, resampling, or a calibrated-per-group scoring rule).

### Clinic — real disparities
Two clinics stand out.

| Clinic | n | Actual no-show | Recall | Precision |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Clinic 3 (Cardiology-heavy) | 642 | 11.2% | 0.36 | 0.33 |
| Clinic 4 (Pediatrics) | 306 | 35.3% | 0.81 | 0.43 |

Clinic 4 has the highest no-show rate and the highest recall — the model works
well there. Clinic 3 has the lowest no-show rate and the lowest recall (36%);
in production this would mean specialty-care no-shows at Clinic 3 are
under-flagged and their slots are less likely to be recovered.

### Scholarship / social-program status — needs attention
Patients on the scholarship flag (612 of 6,400) show:

- **Higher recall** — 0.77 vs. 0.59 for non-scholarship
- **Higher selection rate** — 39.4% assigned to High risk vs. 20.0% overall
- **A calibration gap of +4.7 pp** — the model predicts 28.4% average risk vs.
  a 23.7% actual no-show rate for this group

The recall difference is *good news for equal opportunity of intervention* —
this group is not being missed. But the model is **over-predicting risk** for
scholarship patients relative to their actual behavior. In an assistive-only
setting that means more well-intentioned but unnecessary reminder calls; in
any other context (e.g. if the intervention became allocative or punitive) the
same pattern would be actively harmful. Explicit flag: this feature should be
audited for whether it is acting as a proxy for socioeconomic status generally
and whether the model should be re-fit without it.

### Risk category — bands are well-calibrated internally
The Low / Medium / High bands segment risk cleanly on the test set:

| Category | Share | Actual no-show |
|---|---:|---:|
| Low | 51.4% | 10.2% |
| Medium | 26.7% | 20.2% |
| High | 21.8% | 40.2% |

Each band's average predicted risk sits within ±3.3 pp of the band's actual
no-show rate. The `flag_recall_gap` values for Low and High rows in
`fairness_by_risk_category.csv` are structural (recall is 0 for Low by
definition and 1.0 for High) and are not disparities.

## Overall assessment

- The model shows **no material gender disparity** on the synthetic test set.
- The model shows a **clear age disparity**: recall for seniors is roughly
  one-third of recall for younger patients.
- The model shows **clinic-level disparities** driven partly by base-rate
  differences but partly by the model's calibration inside each clinic.
- The model **over-predicts risk for the scholarship subgroup** by ~5 pp,
  which is not harmful in an assistive-only setting but is a signal that the
  feature warrants scrutiny.
- The Low/Medium/High **risk bands are well-calibrated overall**, which is
  what makes the current operational workflow defensible even with the age
  and clinic gaps.

## Explicit non-claims

- This audit **does not** claim the model is fair.
- This audit **does not** claim the model is production-ready.
- This audit **does not** replace a full pre-deployment fairness review on
  real, representative data with legal/compliance sign-off.
- The results are on synthetic data and **do not generalize** to any specific
  health system's patient population.
- Only measured axes were examined. Other potentially relevant axes (e.g.
  language, insurance type, neighborhood deprivation index) are not
  captured in the synthetic dataset.

## What a full audit would add

The follow-up work described in `docs/model_card.md` §*Future fairness audit*
still stands: language, insurance, and neighborhood-deprivation slices;
subgroup-specific decision thresholds; explicit tests for proxy variables; and
a bootstrapped confidence interval on every subgroup metric so tiny subgroups
(here, the 0-12 and 13-17 age bands) are not over-interpreted.
